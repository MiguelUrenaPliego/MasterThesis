\renewcommand{\sectionnametext}{Traditional seismic risk assessment workflow}
\renewcommand{\headingtextR}{\thesection. \sectionnametext}
\section{\sectionnametext}

The traditional approach is based on full-field surveys, limited detailed structural modelling, and manual interpretation of building typologies. The workflow is structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Full field survey of all buildings (100\% coverage)} using a combination of skilled survey technicians, field assistants, and supervising engineers.
    
    \item \textbf{Detailed structural inspection subset (40 buildings per city)}:
    selected as representative samples for Finite Element (FEM) modelling and nonlinear analysis.

    \item \textbf{Manual data processing and GIS database construction}, including:
    - cleaning and validation of field data  
    - georeferencing and attribute structuring  
    - photo annotation and QA review  

    \item \textbf{Engineering interpretation and typology definition}, where structural engineers:
    - define building typologies per city  
    - assign capacity curves based on observed structural characteristics  
    - extrapolate vulnerability parameters to full building stock  

    \item \textbf{FEM modelling of representative buildings (40 models per city)}:
    includes geometry idealization, meshing, nonlinear pushover analysis, and capacity curve extraction.

    \item \textbf{Reporting and documentation}, including:
    - seismic risk maps  
    - vulnerability classification  
    - final technical report preparation
\end{itemize}

\renewcommand{\sectionnametext}{Hybrid digital twin workflow}
\renewcommand{\headingtextR}{\thesection. \sectionnametext}
\section{\sectionnametext}

The hybrid digital twin approach developed in this thesis replaces full-field manual assessment with a data-driven pipeline based on sparse field sampling, computer vision classification, and cloud-based digital twin generation.

The workflow is structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Field sampling survey (5\% coverage)} using car-based rapid inspection.
This phase collects geolocated imagery and minimal structural descriptors using survey technicians and field assistants.
No full enumeration of buildings is required.

\item \textbf{Computer vision model training (cloud-based)}:
A deep learning model is trained for building classification and structural system recognition using the sampled dataset.
This includes:
- model training (10 hours GPU time)
- feature engineering and dataset preparation
- validation and accuracy calibration

\item \textbf{Per-building inference (full building stock)}:
The trained vision model is executed for every building in the city to segment the building footprint and humans revise the result.

\item \textbf{Additional machine learning models (non-vision)}:
Supporting ML models are applied for:
- capacity curve generation
- structural system estimation

\item \textbf{Finite Element modelling of representative subset (40 buildings per city)}:
A small sample is selected for high-fidelity FEM analysis, including:
- geometry idealization
- nonlinear pushover analysis
- capacity curve extraction
These results are used for calibration of ML predictions.

\item \textbf{Digital twin construction (cloud-based)}:
A city-scale digital twin is generated, integrating:
- geospatial building database
- structural classification outputs
- vulnerability layers
- FEM-calibrated response models

\item \textbf{Web platform deployment and visualization}:
Instead of a static report, outputs are deployed as an interactive system:
- web-based seismic risk explorer
- building-level query system
- scenario simulation interface
- API access for external integration

\item \textbf{Final outputs}:
- interactive digital twin platform  
- structured geospatial database  
- calibrated vulnerability model  
- FEM validation dataset  
- no manual typology assignment required

\end{itemize}

The results clearly show that the hybrid digital twin approach provides a substantial and consistent cost advantage across all three pilot cities. While the traditional workflow scales approximately linearly with the number of buildings due to the need for full-field surveying, manual interpretation, and extensive engineering effort, the hybrid approach decouples most cost components from full population size through the use of sparse field sampling and automated inference. As a result, its scaling behaviour is significantly more favourable for large urban areas. This is reflected in cost reductions between 84\% and 90\%, corresponding to overall cost savings of approximately 6× to 10× depending on city size and labour cost structure (table \ref{tab:cost_comparison_by_city}). The gap widens further in larger cities, demonstrating that the hybrid method becomes increasingly economically advantageous as the spatial scale of application increases.

This approach has the potential to represent a paradigm shift for developing countries in seismic risk assessment. By drastically reducing the cost and computational burden of large-scale vulnerability analysis, it enables a transition from predominantly reactive post-event damage assessment to a proactive, pre-event risk management strategy. In this framework, seismic risk can be continuously evaluated and updated at the urban scale, supporting evidence-based mitigation planning, prioritization of retrofitting interventions, and improved disaster preparedness. As a result, the hybrid digital twin methodology not only improves efficiency but also fundamentally enhances the capacity of resource-constrained regions to manage seismic risk before an event occurs rather than responding only after catastrophic losses have already taken place.
